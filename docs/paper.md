# Transportable survival, non-transportable response: a mechanistic validation of a generative virtual control arm for advanced NSCLC

## Abstract

Virtual control arms—simulated patients that substitute for a trial's concurrent
control group—could reduce control-arm enrollment, but their value depends on
whether simulated patients behave like real ones. We report a
transportability-focused validation of a generative virtual control arm for
advanced non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC). A hierarchical Bayesian model jointly
representing RECIST tumour-size dynamics (bi-exponential tumour-growth inhibition)
and Weibull progression-free and overall survival (PFS, OS) was trained on a
single first-line control arm—the only one of five public datasets releasing
patient-level longitudinal lesion measurements—and evaluated on held-out patients
and on four independent control arms (spanning squamous histology, second-line
therapy, and alternative regimens) reporting only aggregate response and survival.

Internally, simulated PFS and OS were statistically indistinguishable from
held-out real patients (log-rank *p* = 0.63 and 0.54), and the joint model
outperformed a resampling baseline on survival-curve calibration. Externally, the
results split cleanly by endpoint. Survival transported to three of four
independent trials (OS log-rank *p* = 0.18–0.99), including a squamous trial the
non-squamous-trained model had never seen, and failed for one (*p* = 0.018).
RECIST best-overall-response transported to none (χ² *p* ≤ 2×10⁻⁵). Both failures
had identifiable mechanisms. Response non-transport arises because the model
simulates target-lesion size only, missing the non-target and new-lesion
progression that drives much real early progression. OS was under-predicted by
1–2 months because single-trial training gives the survival sub-model no covariate
basis to shift OS between populations, regressing predictions toward the training
marginal (gap versus real OS level: Spearman ρ = +0.80); a landmark analysis
correcting for immortal-time bias excluded subsequent therapy. Each negative
result carries an identified mechanism, and each mechanism a specific fix.

## 1. Introduction

Randomised controlled trials are the evidential foundation of oncology drug
development, but the concurrent control arm they require is costly, slows accrual,
and can raise ethical and feasibility concerns—particularly when patients must be
randomised to a standard of care that a promising experimental agent is expected
to exceed. A *virtual control arm* replaces some or all of that concurrent control
group with simulated patients whose longitudinal disease trajectories and
time-to-event outcomes are generated by a model trained on historical control-arm
data. If the simulated patients behave like real ones, a sponsor can reduce the
size of the physical control arm, run a rapid feasibility or trial-design analysis
before committing to a protocol, or supply a credible synthetic comparator for a
single-arm study. The premise is entirely conditional on that "if": a virtual
control arm is only as trustworthy as the evidence that its simulated patients
reproduce real outcomes, which makes validation—not model sophistication—the
substantive question.

The value of such tools is unevenly distributed. Large developers can build or
license the data infrastructure and modelling expertise that a virtual control arm
demands; investigator-initiated, academic, and smaller-sponsor trials frequently
cannot, even though these are precisely the settings in which control-arm cost and
accrual most often determine whether a trial is feasible at all. A validation
performed entirely on public data, with a fully specified and transparent
methodology, is therefore disproportionately useful to exactly the groups least
able to access proprietary alternatives, independent of whether it matches those
alternatives in scale.

Several established efforts already occupy this space. Commercial and academic
groups—among them Certara's model-based simulation and meta-analytic work [3] and
Unlearn.AI's prognostic "digital twin" methodology [2]—have built mature platforms
for synthetic-control and covariate-adjustment applications, and digital-twin and
external-control approaches have been evaluated in regulatory-facing settings [4].
These are enterprise-oriented systems, commonly built on proprietary patient-level
datasets and validated within them. We make no claim to compete with them on
scale, indication breadth, or regulatory maturity. Our contribution is
intentionally narrower and different in kind: a fully public-data,
end-to-end-transparent validation of one generative model in one indication, whose
purpose is to characterise *where and why* a virtual control arm transports rather
than to demonstrate that it does.

Concretely, we trained a hierarchical Bayesian joint model of RECIST tumour
dynamics and Weibull survival on a single first-line NSCLC control arm—the only
one of five public control-arm datasets releasing patient-level longitudinal
lesion measurements—and evaluated it both on held-out patients from that arm and
on four independent control arms reporting only aggregate outcomes. The central
finding is a clean separation by endpoint. Progression-free and overall survival
transported to independent external trials, including a squamous-histology trial
the non-squamous-trained model had never seen; RECIST best-overall-response
classification transported to none. Crucially, neither result is left as an
unexplained gap. Response non-transport arises because the model simulates
target-lesion tumour size alone and cannot generate the non-target and new-lesion
progression that defines much of real early RECIST progression; the survival
under-prediction arises because a single training trial gives the survival
sub-model no covariate basis to move the outcome level between populations, so its
predictions regress toward the training marginal. Each mechanism, in turn, implies
a specific and distinct remedy.

We emphasise the response result as a methodological contribution rather than a
dataset-specific limitation. Target-lesion-only virtual control arms share a
structural feature—they model the measurable tumour burden that drives RECIST
partial response and stable disease, but not the categorical progression events
(new lesions, unequivocal non-target progression) that RECIST 1.1 [1] also
counts—and this feature predicts a characterisable failure mode for response
classification wherever the approach is used, not only here. Reporting a
mechanistically transparent failure of this kind is, we argue, more useful to the
field than a headline claim of successful transport: it tells a prospective user
of a virtual control arm which endpoints they may rely on and which they may not,
and why.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 specifies the
indication and endpoints, the five-trial public cohort, the
single-trial-training/multi-trial-external-validation design and its
data-availability rationale, the generative model, and the validation metrics.
Section 3 reports internal held-out validation and external aggregate validation
and dissects the two mechanisms. Section 4 discusses the two corresponding fixes,
the generality of the response failure mode, the separate question of real-world
(non-trial) calibration, the remaining limitations, and why mechanistic
transparency is the intended contribution.

## 2. Methods

**2.1 Indication and endpoints.** The target population is advanced or metastatic
NSCLC (stage IIIB/IV), restricted to the comparator (control) arms of phase II/III
trials. The primary generative object is the longitudinal RECIST 1.1 [1] sum of
longest diameters (SLD) of target lesions, in millimetres, over time. The
validated endpoints are progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS);
a landmark binary indicator of progression by six months, used for calibration;
and, for the external trials, the aggregate best overall response (BOR;
CR/PR/SD/PD). Candidate covariates are age, sex, ECOG performance status, stage,
histology (squamous vs non-squamous), smoking, prior therapy lines, baseline SLD,
target-lesion count, and EGFR/ALK/PD-L1 biomarkers where available. NSCLC was
chosen because Project Data Sphere's comparator-arm holdings and RECIST measurement
quality are strongest in this indication; prostate cancer is frequently
bone-predominant and non-measurable by RECIST, and breast cancer is strongly
receptor-stratified. Colorectal cancer was the pre-specified fallback and was not
triggered.

**2.2 Data sources and cohort.** Two real data sources are used. Project Data
Sphere (PDS) [5] supplied five de-identified NSCLC control-arm datasets; because
PDS shares the comparator arm only, each dataset is effectively single-arm, and all
patient-level files are governed by a data-use agreement and are never committed to
the repository. ClinicalTrials.gov supplied 162 arm-level historical PFS/OS medians
for the indication, used solely as a plausibility backstop. A third source, SEER
[12], is loaded but dormant and is not incorporated here (§4). The five cohorts are
summarised in Table M1. Censoring and derivation conventions differ across trials
and bear on interpretation: PFS in trials 438 and 141 is censored at the start of
subsequent anti-cancer therapy; trial 272 uses the ADaM `adtte` derivation; trial
133 (SDTM) carries no populated progression date, so PFS is not derived for it and
only OS is available (from `DM.RFENDY` plus a death disposition); and trial 108
(legacy CRF) has OS and PFS re-derived from end-of-study, follow-up, death,
last-contact, and progression fields. For trial 108 the histology, stage, and
response code lists were not shipped and were inferred from code frequencies, then
cross-checked against the trial's published ~43% squamous and ~79% stage-IV
distributions.

**2.3 Training and validation design.** Only trial 438 carries patient-level RECIST
lesion trajectories; the remaining four supply BOR and PFS/OS aggregates only. We
therefore train and internally validate on trial 438, and reserve the other four
for external aggregate validation. This split was data-driven—dictated by the
availability of lesion-level SLD, not chosen as a methodological preference. It is
nonetheless a demanding test: it asks whether a model that never observed a trial's
patients can reproduce that trial's reported aggregate outcomes from a synthetic
population matched to its baseline covariates. Its cost is that between-trial
heterogeneity can be assessed only at the aggregate level and, more consequentially,
that the model's covariate support is narrow (§4).

**2.4 Generative model.** The primary model (`TGISurvivalModel`, implemented in
NumPyro with NUTS) is a hierarchical Bayesian joint model in the pharmacometric
tumour-growth-inhibition/overall-survival (TGI–OS) tradition [6,7]. The
longitudinal sub-model is a Stein bi-exponential TGI curve with baseline SLD as the
anchor $y_{0,i}$:

$$\mathrm{SLD}_i(t) = y_{0,i}\left(e^{-d_i t} + e^{g_i t} - 1\right),\qquad
  \log \mathrm{SLD}^{obs}_{ij} \sim \mathrm{Normal}\!\left(\log \mathrm{SLD}_i(t_{ij}),\, \sigma_{obs}\right),$$

with hierarchical, covariate-dependent shrinkage and regrowth rates in non-centred
form, $\log d_i = \mu_d + X_i^\top\beta_d + \sigma_d z^d_i$ and
$\log g_i = \mu_g + X_i^\top\beta_g + \sigma_g z^g_i$. The time-to-event sub-model
is a Weibull proportional-hazards model [8] coupled to tumour growth:

$$\mathrm{scale}^E_i = \exp\!\big(a_E + X_i^\top\gamma_E + \theta_E(\log g_i - \mu_g)\big),\qquad
  T^E_i \sim \mathrm{Weibull}\!\left(\mathrm{scale}^E_i, k_E\right),\ E\in\{\text{PFS},\text{OS}\}.$$

Sharing the growth log-rate $\log g_i$ across both likelihoods is what makes the
model joint; a negative $\theta_E$ (faster-growing tumours progress and die sooner)
is the expected sign and is recovered on the real trial-438 data. The baseline
comparator (`MarginalResamplingModel`) resamples donor SLD trajectories within
coarse covariate strata and inverts stratum-level Kaplan–Meier curves for event
times; the Bayesian model must beat it on held-out metrics to justify its added
complexity. To simulate a patient from a covariate vector we draw random effects
from the fitted population, evaluate the TGI trajectory with observation noise, and
draw PFS/OS from the coupled Weibull; because the regrowth term is unbounded and the
growth-rate random effect is log-normal with a heavy upper tail, the growth exponent
is clipped (as at fit time) and simulated SLD is capped at a physiological maximum
(1000 mm), which prevents a few long-horizon draws from overflowing without shifting
the trajectory median. Four modelling assumptions are flagged as refinement
targets: baseline SLD is treated as a known anchor rather than a latent quantity
with measurement error; PFS and OS are separate Weibulls with OS ≥ PFS not enforced;
censoring is assumed independent of the event process given covariates; and the
growth-to-survival link is linear in $\log g_i$.

**2.5 Validation metrics.** Internal validation is patient-level on the trial-438
held-out set. For PFS and OS we compare simulated and real Kaplan–Meier curves with
a log-rank test [9] and a median comparison, subjecting the simulated latent event
times to the test set's empirical censoring so that the comparison is fair; we also
report landmark calibration (expected calibration error, ECE), the time-dependent
inverse-probability-of-censoring-weighted (IPCW) Brier score [10],
predictive-interval coverage, and, for SLD, the continuous ranked probability score
(CRPS) [11]. Splitting is always on patients, never on individual measurements.
External validation is aggregate. For each trial we construct a matched synthetic
population—the trial's real baseline covariates, with a tumour-burden anchor
(baseline SLD and target-lesion count) resampled from trial 438 because the external
trials do not record it—simulate it with the trial-438-trained model, and compare
aggregates to the real trial. BOR is assigned by a RECIST best-response rule applied
to the model's noise-free expected trajectory (per-visit CR/PR/SD/PD with precedence
CR > PR > SD > PD, assessment stopping at the first progression), using target-lesion
SLD only and imposing no confirmation requirement (§3.3, §4). BOR distributions are
compared by a χ² test (Fisher's exact when cells are sparse) over the evaluable
CR/PR/SD/PD counts, and PFS/OS curves by log-rank with the simulated latent times
censoring-matched to the real cohort. Throughout, a large p-value is the desired
result: it indicates that simulated and real aggregates are not detectably
different.

## 3. Results

We trained the joint tumour-growth-inhibition (TGI) and survival model on the one
control arm that released patient-level lesion trajectories (trial 438, a
first-line non-squamous NSCLC arm; the other four datasets released aggregate
response and survival only). We then asked two questions in sequence: whether the
model's simulated patients reproduce real patients held out from that same trial
(internal validation, §3.1), and whether—without ever seeing their patients—the
model reproduces the aggregate outcomes of four independent control arms (external
validation, §3.2). The results separate cleanly by endpoint: survival transports,
RECIST response does not, and each outcome has a distinct, identifiable cause
(§3.3, §3.4).

### 3.1 Internal validation on held-out patients

Of the 179 patients in the training arm, 175 had a measurable baseline target
lesion and were retained; these were split 80/20, stratified by histology, into
140 training and 35 held-out patients. On the held-out set, simulated PFS and OS
Kaplan–Meier curves were statistically indistinguishable from the real curves
(log-rank *p* = 0.63 and 0.54; real versus simulated median PFS 165 versus 142
days, median OS 360 versus 330 days; Fig. 1). Against the resampling baseline, the
joint model was better calibrated on the survival curves (mean log-rank *p* 0.586
versus 0.258) and on tumour-size prediction (SLD CRPS 22.8 versus 26.4 mm), tied
on the inverse-probability-of-censoring-weighted (IPCW) Brier score (0.209), and
slightly worse on landmark calibration error (expected calibration error, ECE,
0.139 versus 0.105; Table 1). This is the same profile observed previously on
synthetic data with a known data-generating process, where the joint model's
advantage was likewise concentrated in survival-curve calibration.

Relative to that synthetic benchmark, every metric degraded on real data (Brier
0.17→0.21, ECE 0.06→0.14, SLD CRPS 11→23 mm; Table 1). We attribute this to
genuinely messier trajectories, the small held-out sample (n = 35), and
over-dispersed SLD predictive intervals: the simulated trajectory median was well
centred (54 mm versus an observed 48 mm at six months) but its intervals were
wide—a real calibration limitation rather than a numerical artefact. As an
external plausibility check, every simulated PFS and OS median, internal and
external, fell within the ClinicalTrials.gov historical range for the indication
(PFS 2.0–16.8 months, OS 6.8–33.9 months), and the internal simulated PFS median
(4.7 months) equalled the historical median.

### 3.2 Survival transports to independent trials; response classification does not

We then tested transportability directly. For each of the four independent
control arms we constructed a synthetic population matched to that trial's real
baseline covariates (with a tumour-burden anchor resampled from the training arm,
since the external trials do not record baseline SLD), simulated it with the model
trained only on trial 438, and compared the simulated aggregates to the trial's
real outcomes. Because these trials report only aggregates, the comparison is
distributional—χ² for best-overall-response (BOR) and log-rank for PFS/OS—where a
large *p*-value indicates that simulated and real are not detectably different.

The results separated cleanly by endpoint (Table 2). **Survival transported to
three of the four trials.** OS was statistically indistinguishable from real for
trials 141, 272, and 133 (log-rank *p* = 0.18, 0.99, and 0.87), and PFS was
indistinguishable wherever it could be derived (*p* = 0.20–0.25; PFS was not
derivable for trial 133, whose data export contains no progression dates).
Transport held even where the training arm's composition should have made it
hardest: trial 272 enrolled exclusively squamous patients and used a different
regimen (gemcitabine–cisplatin), yet its simulated OS matched the real curve
almost exactly (*p* = 0.99; Fig. 2). OS transport failed for a single trial (108,
*p* = 0.018). **RECIST best-overall-response, by contrast, did not transport for
any trial** (χ² *p* ≤ 2×10⁻⁵; Fig. 3). The following two sections dissect each
failure; both have an identifiable mechanism rather than an unexplained gap.

### 3.3 Response non-transport reflects target-lesion-only simulation

The χ² test rejected agreement between simulated and real BOR distributions in
every trial (Table 2), but the failure was structured, not uniform, and its
structure is diagnostic. In the three first-line trials the stable-disease and
partial-response proportions were in fact close—for trial 141, real SD/PR of
46%/40% versus simulated 49%/45%—and the rejection was driven almost entirely by
systematic under-prediction of progressive disease as best response (simulated
1–4% versus real 12–13%). This is the expected consequence of the model's
generative scope. The model simulates only the target-lesion SLD trajectory,
whereas a substantial fraction of real early progression is defined by new lesions
or unequivocal non-target progression—events the model has no mechanism to
produce. (With roughly 500 patients per trial, the χ² test also has power to flag
modest discrepancies.)

The second-line trial (133) failed far more severely (χ² *p* = 2×10⁻⁵⁸) and
qualitatively differently: simulated response was roughly fivefold too high (PR
49% versus real 9%) and PD was again under-predicted (3% versus 42%). This is the
anticipated out-of-domain failure. Training was first-line only, with prior
therapy lines held constant, so the model has no representation of second-line
refractory disease and instead applies first-line shrinkage dynamics to a
population that does not exhibit them. It is not a separate failure mode but the
same one—target-lesion dynamics learned in a single setting—pushed past the
boundary of the training distribution.

### 3.4 Overall-survival under-prediction reflects single-trial covariate support

Simulated OS fell below real OS in all four external trials, by 1.1–2.4 months. We
first pursued the most mechanistically natural explanation. The model represents
tumour growth but has no knowledge of treatment after progression, so if the
external trials delivered more post-progression subsequent therapy than the
training population, real OS would be extended in a way the model could not
reproduce. Subsequent-therapy rates did rise across the external trials (from 25%
to 55%), and a naive within-trial comparison appeared to confirm the hypothesis:
patients who received subsequent therapy lived 7–9 months longer than those who
did not.

That comparison, however, is confounded by immortal-time bias—a patient must
survive to progression and remain fit enough to be re-treated, so the untreated
group is enriched for early deaths by construction. A 180-day landmark analysis,
restricting to patients alive at 180 days and classifying by whether subsequent
therapy had begun by then, **reversed** the direction of the association, with
re-treated patients showing *shorter* subsequent survival (12.3 versus 17.7
months in trial 108; Fig. 4). Consistent with this reversal, the OS gap across trials
showed no dose-response with subsequent-therapy rate—the correlation was slightly
negative (Spearman ρ = −0.20)—and was, at the level of individual trials, if
anything inverted: the largest gap (trial 141, 2.4 months) occurred in the trial
with the *lowest* subsequent-therapy rate and the same regimen as the training
arm, while the trial with the highest rate (272) showed the *best* OS agreement.
We therefore excluded subsequent therapy as the explanation.

The gap instead tracked each trial's true OS *level*: the Spearman correlation
between the OS gap and the real median OS was **+0.80**. The model under-predicted
precisely those trials whose real OS exceeded the training arm's (13.4, 11.7, and
11.1 months) and matched the one that fell below it (9.9 months). This is the
signature of a survival sub-model that cannot move the OS level between
populations. The training arm offered almost no covariate variation for it to
learn from—one regimen, stage IV only, non-squamous only, and no recorded ECOG
performance status—so its predictions regress toward the training arm's marginal
OS (approximately 10.5 months) regardless of the target population. Two further
observations confirm this reading and locate the cause inside the model rather
than in the external cohorts. First, the under-prediction was already present
internally, where the held-out simulated OS median (approximately 10.8 months) sat
below the real value (approximately 11.8 months); a gap visible within the
training trial itself cannot be an artefact of cross-trial transport. Second, the
single significant external failure (trial 108, *p* = 0.018) reflects statistical
power rather than a population-specific effect—its median gap (1.9 months) was
smaller than that of trial 141 (2.4 months, not significant), and it reached
significance only because of its large sample and high event maturity.

## 4. Discussion

This validation produced a clean split by endpoint—survival transported to
independent external control arms, RECIST response did not—and, more importantly,
attached an identified mechanism to each result. The two failures are distinct in
cause and imply distinct, non-interchangeable remedies. Collapsing them into a
generic "more data would help" would obscure the substantive point, so we keep them
separate throughout.

**The OS-compression fix: covariate support, not data volume.** The overall-survival
under-prediction of §3.4 is a covariate-support problem. With a single training arm
offering almost no covariate variation—one regimen, stage IV only, non-squamous
only, and no recorded ECOG performance status—the survival sub-model has no basis on
which to shift the OS *level* between populations, so it regresses every target
cohort toward the training arm's marginal OS (~10.5 months) and under-predicts
longer-lived populations. The remedy is diversity along the axes that actually move
survival: additional lesion-level control arms spanning squamous histology,
second-line therapy, alternative regimens, and ECOG variation, or, failing that, an
explicit study-level random effect that lets the model represent the between-trial
level shifts it currently cannot express. Simply adding more patients from the same
one arm would not help; the constraint is the *span* of the training covariates, not
their count.

**The BOR-transport fix: generative scope, not covariate support.** The response
failure of §3.3 is a generative-scope problem and is orthogonal to the survival fix.
The model simulates the target-lesion SLD trajectory only and therefore cannot
produce the non-target progression and new-lesion appearance that define a large
share of real early RECIST progression. The remedy is to extend the generative
object—model non-target disease and new-lesion incidence, and add a RECIST
confirmation rule—so that simulated progression is not target-SLD-only. No quantity
of additional survival covariates addresses this, and no amount of lesion-scope
modelling addresses the OS compression; the two fixes are independent and must be
pursued as such.

**A general failure mode for target-lesion-only virtual control arms.** We think the
response result is worth stating as a general finding rather than a local one. Any
virtual control arm that models measurable tumour burden alone shares the same
structural gap with respect to RECIST 1.1 [1]: it can reproduce partial response and
stable disease, which are size-defined, but it cannot reproduce the categorical
progression events—new lesions, unequivocal non-target progression—that RECIST also
counts and that drive much early progressive disease. The signature we observed,
systematic under-prediction of PD-as-best-response with SD and PR proportions close
and the χ² rejection concentrated almost entirely in the PD cell, is therefore a
predictable, characterisable failure mode of the modelling approach itself, not an
artefact of this dataset or these particular trials. A prospective user should
expect target-lesion-only synthetic response distributions to be optimistic and
should not rely on them for response-based endpoints without explicit
non-target/new-lesion modelling. That the failure is mechanistic and directional
(optimistic, PD-concentrated) rather than random is what makes it both diagnosable
and, in principle, correctable.

**Real-world calibration is a separate, out-of-scope direction.** The present work is
a *trial-data* validation: every comparator here is itself a trial control arm, and
trial populations are healthier and more selected than the general metastatic-NSCLC
population. Whether the model calibrates to real-world, non-trial survival is a
different question that requires a population-level reference such as SEER [12], and
it is explicitly out of scope here. We regard SEER-based calibration as an
independent future layer rather than a variant of either fix above: it addresses
population representativeness, whereas the two fixes address covariate support and
generative scope, respectively.

**Limitations beyond the two headline mechanisms.** A candid accounting extends past
the two mechanisms. First, the OS-transport evidence rests on a *single*
lesion-level training trial. The level-tracking correlation (Spearman ρ = +0.80
between the OS gap and each trial's real median OS), the gap's presence already
inside the training trial, and the exclusion of subsequent therapy together make the
covariate-support account well-supported, but establishing it causally requires a
second lesion-level training arm—the same arm the OS fix would supply—so the
mechanism, though well-evidenced, is not yet proven. Second, trial 133's data export
contains no populated progression date, so PFS could not be derived there and its
transport evidence is OS-only. Third, trial 108 required inference of histology,
stage, and response code lists from code frequencies (cross-checked against the
trial's published ~43% squamous and ~79% stage-IV distributions), a legacy-format
caveat that should temper the weight placed on trial 108 specifically—though we note
that 108 is the one trial where OS transport was rejected, and that its rejection is
attributable to statistical power rather than to a population effect (§3.4). Fourth,
the model embeds simplifications flagged as refinement targets—separate PFS and OS
Weibulls with no enforced OS ≥ PFS ordering, baseline SLD treated as a known anchor
rather than a latent quantity carrying measurement error, and a growth-to-survival
link that is linear in $\log g_i$—each a potential contributor to residual
miscalibration that we have not isolated.

**Why mechanistic transparency is the contribution.** The result here is not a clean
"it works." It is a validation in which one endpoint transports, another does not,
and both behaviours are explained. We regard that as the more useful outcome. A
virtual control arm reported only as successful tells a prospective user nothing
about the conditions under which it will mislead them; a virtual control arm whose
failure modes are located and mechanistically explained tells them exactly which
endpoints to trust, in which populations, and what would have to change to extend
that trust. For a tool intended to substitute for real control patients—where an
unexamined failure could bias a trial's read-out—that transparency is not a
consolation prize for an incomplete positive result. It is the point.

## Figures

**Figure 1. Internal validation on held-out trial-438 patients (*n* = 35).**
Kaplan–Meier overlays of simulated versus real (a) progression-free and (b) overall
survival for the 20% of trial 438 held out from training, with simulated latent
event times subjected to the held-out set's empirical censoring for a fair
comparison. The curves are statistically indistinguishable (log-rank *p* = 0.63 for
PFS, 0.54 for OS; real vs simulated median PFS 165 vs 142 days, median OS 360 vs 330
days). Panel (c) shows a representative simulated SLD trajectory with its predictive
interval against observed target-lesion measurements. Source artifacts:
`results/real_data/figures/438_internal_bayesian_tgi_survival_pfs_survival.png`,
`..._os_survival.png`, `..._sld_example.png`.

**Figure 2. External survival transport across four independent control arms.**
Per-trial Kaplan–Meier overlays of simulated versus real overall survival (all four
trials) and progression-free survival (trials 141, 272, 108; not derivable for trial
133), from synthetic populations matched to each trial's baseline covariates and
simulated with the trial-438-trained model. Simulated latent times are
censoring-matched to the real cohort; curves compared by log-rank. OS is
statistically indistinguishable from real for trials 141, 272, and 133 (*p* = 0.18,
0.99, 0.87) and rejected for trial 108 (*p* = 0.018); PFS is indistinguishable
wherever derivable (*p* = 0.20–0.25). Sample sizes: 141 *n* = 467, 272 *n* = 549,
133 *n* = 455, 108 *n* = 532. Source artifacts:
`results/real_data/external/figures/{141,272,133,108}_os_km.png` and `..._pfs_km.png`.

**Figure 3. External best-overall-response transport.** Simulated versus real BOR
proportions (CR/PR/SD/PD) for each of the four external trials, compared by χ² test
(Fisher's exact when cells are sparse) over the evaluable categories. Agreement is
rejected for every trial (*p* ≤ 2×10⁻⁵). In the three first-line trials the
rejection is driven by under-prediction of PD-as-best-response (simulated 1–4% vs
real 12–13%), with SD and PR proportions close (e.g. trial 141: real SD/PR 46%/40%
vs simulated 49%/45%); the second-line trial 133 fails severely and qualitatively
differently (simulated PR 49% vs real 9%; PD 3% vs 42%). Sample sizes as in Fig. 2.
Source artifacts: `results/real_data/external/figures/{141,272,133,108}_bor.png`.

**Figure 4 (supporting). Immortal-time bias in the subsequent-therapy analysis
(trial 108).** Median overall survival by subsequent-therapy status under (a) a
naive split on ever-receipt of subsequent therapy and (b) a 180-day landmark
restricting to patients alive at 180 days and classifying by whether subsequent
therapy had begun by then. The naive split shows re-treated patients apparently
living far longer (15.8 vs 6.9 months), whereas the landmark analysis reverses the
direction (12.3 vs 17.7 months), demonstrating that the naive association is an
artefact of survival selection rather than a treatment effect and supporting the
exclusion of subsequent therapy as the cause of OS under-prediction (§3.4). This
is a presentation figure: naive medians are read from the committed diagnostic
(`results/real_data/subsequent_therapy_diagnostic.csv`), while the landmark medians
are the documented trial-108 result (`docs/methodology.md` §4.2), recomputed by
`scripts/diagnose_os_subsequent_therapy.py` from the DUA-protected patient-level
export (not committed, hence not re-run here). Source artifact:
`results/real_data/figures/108_subsequent_therapy_landmark.png`, generated by
`scripts/plot_fig4_landmark_reversal.py`.
